What are we trying to do?

You are about to help out at an OpenTechSchool workshop as a coach. With this presentation we want to give you a quick introduction of what that means.

The focus for the event is to show learners that coding and tech is nothing to be afraid of but is a lot of fun.

Introduction to Coaching

This OTS coaching style has its root in the RailsBridge teaching style. As such it is very focussed on creating a friendly and open environment for learners of all sorts.

How to achieve this and have a pleasant learning experience is focus of this presentation.

The Material

We strongly believe the best way to learn to code - or any other tech related topic for that matter - is by hands-on, experience oriented and pragmatic workshop worked through on ones own pace. Our curricula are designed for exactly that purpose.

That is also reflected in the way we organise ourself on sight: there is no class-room-style setting, everyone has their own workspace instead. There is no teacher in front and there is no one going through the material with a presenter.

Coaching ain't Teaching

In that regard we are not standing in front and teach here. Coaches

  • Stand by on the sideline
  • Are right there when needed
  • Focus on the learners
  • Have sympathy for their (in-)abilities
  • Encourage to go further through motivation
  • And ensure one has fun doing it.

Creating an friendly environment

  • Smile
  • Make eye contact
  • Admit your ignorance
  • Be kind and friendly
  • Use their name (we all have name tags)
  • Tell people it is ok to make mistakes
  • Tell people to take breaks when it gets frustrating

Creating an friendly environment part 2

  • Assume that anyone you're teaching to has zero knowledge but infinite intelligence
  • Encourage learners to ask questions
  • Respond positively
  • Use normal language over jargon
  • Look around whether someone else might be having troubles
  • Make sure whether the learner understood what you said
  • And explain it again - differently - if not the case yet.

Questions are good!

  • Get people comfortable asking questions
  • Ask for questions - even dumb ones
  • Give other learners the possibility to try to answer that question
  • Coding is collaboration - make sure the learners understand that

Responding to questions

Chances are, there is a certain question when the learner calls you to help them. How do you respond?

  • Positevly:
    • i’m glad you said that
    • what an interesting question
    • great question
  • If in doubt: blame the material, never the learner

Pacing

  • This is not about you, but the learner, we go by their pace
  • Everyone learns at their own pace - and that is a good thing
  • Talk sssssslllllloooooowwwwwwllllllyyyyyyyy.
  • Wait much longer than you feel is comfortable for questions/comments (count to 10 by yourself)

Don'ts

a few things we are not doing

Don'ts

  • We do not hit on anyone
  • We do not roll our eyes on questions
  • We do not use the time to advertise our own company/job/ourself
  • We do not pick or make fun of anyone or anything (not even PHP!)
  • We do not discuss any programming language, methods or technologies for that matter

We do not discuss programming languages

It is just confusing to learners: In the tech community we are having some strong opinions and our ways to express those. But for people new to it this can quickly look like a huge fight. And we do not fight each other.

We are here to coach you in this programming language/method/technology and that is the focus for this event.
